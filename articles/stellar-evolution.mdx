# Stellar evolution

The basic logic of how stars operate is simple and almost circular: a star is a fight between gravity trying to crush it and the internal pressure from nuclear fusion holding it up. Almost everything about how a star lives, what it creates, and how it dies follows from how that fight evolves as the fuel runs out or changes.

## Birth

<Img src="articles/milky-way.jpg" alt="Milky Way galaxy" float="right" />

Stars form from nebulae – cold, diffuse clouds of mostly hydrogen and helium left over from the Big Bang and from previous stellar deaths. Small local differences in density cause a region of the cloud to begin contracting under its own weight. As gas falls inward, the increasing pressure makes it heat up, and over hundreds of thousands to a few million years the centre of the collapsing region reaches about 10 million kelvin.

At that temperature, protons in hydrogen nuclei are moving fast enough that quantum tunnelling lets them occasionally overcome their electromagnetic repulsion and fuse. The released energy ignites sustained fusion and halts further collapse, the object is now a star.

A minimum mass is needed for this to happen at all. Below roughly 0.08 solar masses (8% the mass of the Sun), the contracting body never gets hot enough in its core for hydrogen fusion to begin. Instead, it becomes a brown dwarf, glowing faintly from leftover gravitational heat. Above about 150 solar masses, radiation pressure becomes so strong that the star tears itself apart before it can stabilize. Almost everything we see in the night sky lies between these two limits.

## Main sequence

Once hydrogen fusion is running stably in the core, the star enters the main sequence, the phase where it spends roughly 90% of its life and where most stars are found at any given moment.

Four hydrogen nuclei (each a single proton) fuse into one helium nucleus (two protons, two neutrons), releasing energy that pushes outward and balances gravity. Two different pathways accomplish this net reaction:

- In lower-mass stars like the Sun, the **proton-proton chain** dominates: protons fuse directly with one another.
- In more massive, hotter stars with core temperatures above about 17 million kelvin, the **CNO cycle** takes over. Pre-existing carbon, nitrogen, and oxygen act as catalysts, shuttling protons through a loop that arrives at the same net result. Nitrogen from the cycle starts accumulating because the step that transforms it is the slowest. The Sun gets only about 1% of its energy from the CNO cycle; very massive stars get nearly all of theirs from it.

This balance between gravity pulling in and fusion pushing out is very stable and it lasts until the core runs out of hydrogen. How long that takes depends on the mass of the star. More massive stars are hotter and denser, and burn fuel orders of magnitude faster than their larger reserves can compensate for, thus living much shorter lives:

- A red dwarf of 0.2 solar masses can stay on the main sequence for trillions of years – longer than the current age of the universe.
- The Sun, (with 1 solar mass obviously), has a main sequence lifetime of about 10 billion years, of which about 4.6 billion have already passed.
- A 25-solar-mass blue giant burns through its hydrogen in only about 7 million years.

## Giant phase

When hydrogen in the core runs out, fusion there stops. With nothing pushing outward, the core further contracts under its own weight, and the gravitational energy heats the surrounding shell of unburnt hydrogen until *that* layer ignites. Shell fusion is more intense than core fusion was, and the additional energy inflates the outer layers of the star enormously. The star becomes a red giant, or, for stars above roughly 8 solar masses, a red supergiant – swollen and relatively cool at the surface.

Inside, the contracting core eventually reaches about 100 million kelvin, which is hot enough to fuse helium into carbon in the **triple-alpha process**. Two helium nuclei first fuse into beryllium-8, which is so unstable it almost always falls apart again within a fraction of a second. Only when a third helium nucleus happens to strike the beryllium during that brief window does the chain continue, producing stable carbon.

In stars below about 8 solar masses, fusion essentially stops at this step. The core ends up being made of carbon and oxygen and has no further fuel it can ignite.

More massive stars keep going. Each time the current fuel runs out in the core, the core contracts, heats further, and ignites the next fusion stage in an onion shell structure:

- hydrogen → helium
- helium → carbon and oxygen
- carbon → neon, magnesium, sodium
- neon → oxygen, magnesium
- oxygen → silicon, sulfur
- silicon → iron

Each successive stage releases less energy per reaction than the one before, and the core must burn fuel faster to support the star's weight. For a 25-solar-mass star:

- Hydrogen burns ~7 million years
- Helium burns ~700 000 years
- Carbon burns ~600 years
- Neon burns ~1 year
- Oxygen burns ~6 months
- Silicon burns ~1 day

The star spends millions of years quietly fusing hydrogen, but the final transformation from silicon to iron takes a single day.

The chain stops at iron because iron-56 sits near the peak of the nuclear binding energy curve. Fusing it would consume energy rather than release it. Nickel-62 and iron-58 are technically more tightly bound, but the chain of reactions in the star's core does not produce them directly. What actually accumulates is nickel-56, which decays through cobalt-56 into iron-56 over weeks.

## End of life

What happens to the star at this point depends on its mass.

### Low to intermediate mass: white dwarves

Stars below about 8 solar masses end the giant phase by shedding their outer layers in relatively gentle stellar winds and pulsations, exposing the bare carbon-oxygen core. The expelled gas, briefly lit by the hot core, forms a planetary nebula – misleadingly named, since they have nothing to do with planets.

The exposed core is a **white dwarf**: about the mass of the Sun packed into the volume of Earth, no longer fusing anything, held up against further collapse by **electron degeneracy pressure** – a quantum-mechanical effect where electrons resist being forced into the same state. With no fuel left, the white dwarf simply radiates away its leftover heat. The cooling process takes trillions of years; the universe is not yet old enough to contain a fully cooled white dwarf.

A white dwarf cannot exceed about 1.4 solar masses (the **Chandrasekhar limit**), the mass at which electron degeneracy pressure fails. Above this, the object collapses. The rising temperature ignites runaway carbon fusion throughout the star, and the entire white dwarf detonates as a Type Ia supernova (described below), leaving nothing behind. An isolated white dwarf cannot reach this point on its own, it requires a companion to feed it extra mass. Type Ia supernovae produce most of the iron in the universe and, because they explode at predictable brightness, are used as cosmological distance markers.

### High mass: supernovae

Stars above about 8 solar masses end differently. Once silicon burning has built an inert iron core in the centre, that core grows from continued shell burning around it. When the iron core itself exceeds the Chandrasekhar limit, electron degeneracy pressure fails. The core collapses in well under a second, from roughly the size of Earth to a sphere about 20 kilometers across, before the rest of the star has time to react.

The collapse only stops when the core reaches densities at which neutrons themselves resist further compression. The falling outer layers of the star slam into this suddenly rigid core and rebound outward in a shock wave that, with help from a flood of neutrinos pouring out of the new core, blows the rest of the star apart. This is a **core-collapse supernova**, brilliant enough to briefly outshine an entire galaxy. The expelled material seeds the interstellar medium with the heavier elements built up during the star's life and during the explosion itself.

What's left behind depends on how much mass the collapse retains:

- If the remnant is below about 2-2.5 solar masses (the **Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff limit**), it remains a **neutron star** – essentially one giant atomic nucleus, a city-sized sphere of degenerate neutrons.
- If the remnant exceeds that limit, no known force can support it. It collapses to a **black hole**.

Very roughly, initial stellar masses of about 8–20 solar masses produce neutron stars, and masses above about 20–25 solar masses produce black holes, though the boundary depends on metallicity, rotation, and how much mass the star loses to stellar winds beforehand.

## The picture by initial mass

The whole life cycle reduces to what the star weighed when it formed.

- Initial mass &lt; 0.08 M☉
  - never ignites
  - end state: brown dwarf.
- 0.08–0.5 M☉
  - main sequence: trillions of years
  - end state: red dwarf, slowly cools (in principle eventually a white dwarf)
- 0.5–8 M☉
  - main sequence: hundreds of millions to ~50 billion years
  - end state: red giant → planetary nebula → white dwarf
- 8–20 M☉
  - main sequence: tens of millions of years
  - end state: red supergiant → core-collapse supernova → neutron star
- &gt; 20 M☉
  - main sequence: a few million years
  - end state: red supergiant → core-collapse supernova → black hole

Everything else, the specific elements produced, the brightness and colour, the spectral lines, follows from where on this table the star sits and which stage of fuel it is currently working through.
