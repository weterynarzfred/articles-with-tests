# The history of the universe

<div className="date">2026-06-03</div>

Dating units:
- **Ga** – gigaannum, a billion years ago,
- **Ma** – megaannum, a million years ago,
- **ka** – kiloannum, a thousand years ago.

Used as a date, "4.6 Ga" means "4.6 billion years ago". Because the universe began 13.8 Ga, an age-ago and a time-after-the-beginning are just two ways of naming the same instant: 4.6 Ga ago is the same moment as 9.2 billion years after the Big Bang. The earliest events are easier to think about as time after the beginning, the later ones as time ago, so this article switches between the two as convenient.

## The Big Bang

**~13.8 Ga** – The universe began in a state of extreme density and temperature and has been expanding and cooling ever since. It is worth being careful about what "Big Bang" means, it's not an explosion of matter into surrounding empty space, but an expansion of space itself, happening everywhere at once. Run the expansion backwards and everything converges, which is why the early universe was so hot and dense. The very first instant is beyond the reach of current theory.

## Inflation

**~10<sup>-32</sup> seconds after the Big Bang** – In a sliver of a second, the universe is thought to have undergone inflation, a burst of exponential expansion that enlarged a subatomic patch to roughly the size of a grapefruit, an increase by a factor of perhaps 10<sup>26</sup>. Inflation neatly explains several otherwise puzzling features: why the universe looks so uniform in every direction, why space is so close to geometrically flat, and where the slight density variations that later seeded galaxies came from. It was caused by tiny quantum fluctuations, stretched to cosmic scale.

## The quark epoch

**~10<sup>-12</sup> to 10<sup>-6</sup> seconds after the Big Bang** – The universe became plasma far too hot for composite particles to hold together. Its matter existed as free quarks and gluons, the constituents that would normally be bound inside protons and neutrons. As it cooled, two things happened. The quarks combined into protons and neutrons, and matter and antimatter, produced in nearly equal amounts, annihilated on contact back into energy.

"Nearly" equal is the key word. For every billion antimatter particles there were about a billion and one regular matter particles, and when the annihilation finished, that tiny surplus was all that remained. Everything material in the universe today is made from that leftover one in a billion difference. Why matter slightly outnumbered antimatter is still an open question.

## Nucleosynthesis

**~3 to 20 minutes after the Big Bang** – A few minutes in, the universe cooled enough for protons and neutrons to fuse and stick, forming the first atomic nuclei in a process called Big Bang nucleosynthesis. There was only a short window before expansion thinned and cooled everything below fusion temperature, so only the lightest nuclei formed: hydrogen, about a quarter of today's helium by mass, and a trace of lithium. Heavier elements appeared only hundreds of millions of years and the first stars.

These were still bare nuclei, not atoms. It was far too hot for electrons to stay attached, so the universe remained an opaque, glowing plasma of nuclei and free electrons. Light couldn't travel far before scattering off a loose electron.

## Recombination and the cosmic microwave background

**~380 thousand years after the Big Bang** – The universe cooled down to a few thousand degrees, cool enough for electrons to finally bind to nuclei and form neutral atoms. This is called the recombination. With the free electrons bound up, light was no longer constantly scattered, and the universe became transparent for the first time.

The light set free at that moment is still traveling today, stretched by the universe's expansion from a hot glow into faint microwaves. We detect it coming from every direction as the cosmic microwave background (CMB), which is the oldest light possible to detect, and a direct snapshot of the universe back then. Its faint temperature variations are the imprint of those primordial density fluctuations, and precise measurements of them are how the age and properties of the universe were pinned down.

## The first stars

<Img src="articles/universe.jpg" float="right" alt="A nebula with gas clouds and bright stars against a dark sky." />

**~100 to 200 million years after the Big Bang** – After recombination comes a long, dark stretch with no stars named the cosmic dark ages. Gravity slowly draws the slightly denser regions of gas together until, after a hundred million years or so, the first clumps grow dense and hot enough to ignite fusion. These first stars (called Population III) formed from pristine hydrogen and helium with no heavier elements at all, which let them grow extremely massive and burn hot, bright, and brief. *(Read more about star current formation in [Stellar Evolution](stellar-evolution).)*

Their intense ultraviolet light stripped electrons back off the neutral hydrogen, ionizing it again. This era, reionization, is why the diffuse hydrogen filling intergalactic space sits as ionized plasma to this day. Neutral hydrogen has not vanished, but it is now the minority; the bulk of it survives where it is shielded and dense, inside galaxies and gas clouds.

## The first galaxies

**~300 to 500 million years after the Big Bang** – Gravity continued to gather stars and gas into the first true galaxies, many already harboring supermassive black holes at their centers. How quickly this happened has recently been reevaluated. The James Webb Space Telescope was launched in 2021 partly to study these early galaxies, and it has found them earlier and brighter than previous models predicted. The current record-holder is a galaxy seen as it was only about 280 million years after the Big Bang. Why galaxies assembled and lit up so fast is an active research question.

## Cosmic noon

**~10 Ga** *(≈3 to 4 billion years after the Big Bang)* – Star formation across the universe was not steady. It climbed to a peak around 10 Ga, an era sometimes called the cosmic noon. Galaxies were forming stars roughly ten times faster than they do now. Most of the stars that have ever existed were born around this period. Since then the cosmic birth rate has been in a long, slow decline as galaxies use up their available gas.

## The Milky Way

**between 12 and ~8 Ga** – Our own galaxy did not appear all at once. Its oldest stars date to within a billion years of the Big Bang, and the galaxy grew over billions of years by pulling in gas and swallowing smaller galaxies. One such merger, with a galaxy nicknamed Gaia-Enceladus, around 10 Ga ago, left a distinct population of stars on tilted orbits that astronomers can still pick out today.

## The Solar System

**~4.6 Ga** – Within the Milky Way a cloud of gas and dust collapsed under its own gravity. Most of the material fell to the center and ignited as the Sun; the rest flattened into a spinning protoplanetary disk, out of which the planets grew by accretion. The cloud was not pristine: it was enriched with heavier elements forged and scattered by earlier generations of stars, which is why the Solar System contains the carbon, oxygen, iron, and other elements that the Big Bang never made. This was not a unique process in any way and the same thing was happening all over the universe.

## The Earth and the Moon

**~4.54 Ga** – The Earth accreted from the disk over a few tens of millions of years. Shortly after it formed, a Mars-sized body collided with the young planet in a glancing impact. The debris thrown into orbit by that collision coalesced into the Moon. This is why Earth and Moon are so similar in composition, and why the Moon is unusually large relative to its planet. From there, over a much longer time, oceans, the first life, and eventually humans formed.
