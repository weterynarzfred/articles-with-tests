# Foxes

<small>*2026-05-29*</small>

Foxes are small to medium-sized members of the dog family, found on every continent except Antarctica. Around twelve species belong to the genus *Vulpes* (the "true foxes") including the red fox, the desert-dwelling fennec, and the Arctic fox. Several other species, like the gray fox of North America, sit in different genera but share the same general body plan and lifestyle.

A fox's signature hunting move is the **mouse pounce**: it locates prey by sound under snow or grass, then leaps high and dives nearly vertically, pinning the animal with its forepaws. Foxes that catch more than they can eat will cache the surplus, burying it shallowly and returning to it later, sometimes days later, relying on memory and scent to find it again.

## Behavior and senses

  Most foxes are <DropZone answer="crepuscular" />, meaning their activity peaks around dawn and dusk. They extend that activity into the night when hunting requires it or when human disturbance makes daytime movement risky, but the twilight hours are when their prey are naturally most active and it balances visibility for the fox with cover from larger predators.

  Their sense of <DropZone answer="hearing" /> is exceptionally sharp. A red fox can detect the squeak of a mouse from over a hundred meters away and pinpoint small movements beneath dense snow cover. This is what makes the pounce viable – the fox locates the target acoustically before it ever sees it.

  The <DropZone answer="Arctic" /> fox is the species best adapted to extreme cold. Its coat is the warmest of any mammal, and it shifts color seasonally, from white in winter, to brown or grey in summer. Its short legs, small ears, and rounded body all reduce heat loss.

  Coats also provide <DropZone answer="camouflage" /> in less extreme habitats: the red fox's rusty coloring blends into autumn undergrowth, the fennec's pale sandy fur into desert.

  Foxes shelter in underground <DropZone answer="dens" />, often called earths. A typical den has multiple entrances and chambers and may be reused across many generations, sometimes excavated by previous occupants like badgers or rabbits rather than dug by the foxes themselves. They are used most intensively when raising cubs; outside the breeding season, adults often simply sleep above ground in dense cover. Adults defend a <DropZone answer="territory" /> marked primarily by scent – urine and droppings deposited along the boundary. Territory size depends heavily on food availability and can range from under a square kilometer in rich habitat to dozens of square kilometers in barren terrain.

  Foxes are highly <DropZone answer="adaptable" />, which is why several species, the red fox in particular, have moved successfully into <DropZone answer="urban" /> areas. City foxes eat much the same diet as rural ones: small mammals, insects, fruit, supplemented by food waste. They tend to have smaller territories and live at higher densities than their countryside counterparts.

## Social life and reproduction

Foxes are less social than wolves or domestic dogs. The basic unit is a breeding pair, though in good habitat a group may include several related adult females sharing a territory and sometimes helping raise each other's young. Cubs are born in spring, typically four to six per litter, and stay with their parents through summer before dispersing in autumn to find their own territories. Both parents bring food to the den.

Communication is rich and varied: more than twenty distinct vocalizations have been documented in red foxes, ranging from the eerie scream of a vixen in heat to soft contact calls between mates. Scent marking and body language carry much of the rest of the social load.

## Ecological role

As mid-sized predators, foxes regulate populations of rodents, rabbits, and ground-nesting birds. In places where larger predators have been removed, they can become the dominant carnivore and shape the ecosystem accordingly. The red fox in particular has been introduced, often deliberately for hunting, to regions where it is not native, most notoriously Australia, where it has contributed to the decline or extinction of several small marsupial species that evolved without comparable predators.
